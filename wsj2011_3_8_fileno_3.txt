European stocks ended a choppy session slightly higher Tuesday, supported by strength in the telecommunications sector, as oil prices took a breather following media reports that Libyan leader Moammar Gadhafi may step down. 

The Stoxx Europe 600 index closed up 0.4% at 281.81, while France's CAC-40 index ended up 0.6% at 4015.91. The U.K.'s FTSE 100 and Germany's DAX both finished nearly flat at 5974.76 and 7164.75, respectively. 

The drop in oil prices helped to lighten the mood, as investors took some comfort from Qatar's oil minister, who said the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries would be prepared to ramp up output if needed. 

Oil prices have been firmly in focus since Middle Eastern and North African unrest began, with investors worried that rising prices might curb consumer spending, threatening global growth prospects. 

In the currency markets, the euro fell against the dollar as concerns about indebted euro-zone countries came to the fore again, ahead of Friday's European summit. By the time of the European stock markets close, the single currency was trading at $1.3746 from $1.3977 late Monday. Sterling last traded at $1.6064 from $1.6205 and the dollar was at 81.78 yen from 82.26 yen.

Late in Europe, light, sweet crude for April delivery was down 30 cents at $105.14 on the New York Mercantile Exchange. Gold for April delivery was down 0.2% at $1,429.20 per troy ounce late in Europe on the Comex division of Nymex.

 In major market action: Telecommunications stocks got a boost after Morgan Stanley raised its stance on the sector to attractive from in line, helping the Stoxx Europe 600 telecoms index to close up 1.8%. The brokerage also raised BT Group and Vodafone to overweight from equalweight, pushing the stocks up 4% and 1.8%, respectively.

Meanwhile, M&A talk surrounding Deutsche Telekom also contributed to the sector's buoyancy. The stock added nearly 4% on reports that U.S.-based Sprint Nextel may buy the company's T-Mobile USA unit. A spokesperson from Deutsche Telekom declined to comment. 

Mining stocks were among the biggest decliners in Europe, as gold prices moved lower. Randgold Resources slumped 8.2% and African Barrick Gold fell 3.6%.

In Frankfurt, UBS lifted Volkswagen to "buy" from "neutral." The auto maker's shares rose 2.3%. Chemical group BASF fell 2.1%, and steelmaker ThyssenKrupp dropped 1.8%.

Tire maker Michelin rose 2.9% and insurer AXA advanced 2.6%, both in Paris.

Energy stocks were also weaker in line with lower oil prices. In London, BG Group and Tullow Oil lost 1.6% and 2.2%, respectively.

Meanwhile, Old Mutual rose 3.3% after the banking and insurance group narrowed its net loss for 2010, and said it was upbeat for 2011.
